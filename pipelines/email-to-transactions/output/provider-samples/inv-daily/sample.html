Word of the Day
Underweight


It is a rating given to a company by financial analysts

An underweight rating is given when an analyst thinks a company's stock is going to perform poorer than initial expectations.

They are basically suggesting that investors should hold less of that stock in their portfolio.

It does not mean the company is bad— it just means that the analysts think the company's stock is less attractive than other stocks in the same sector.

The other two ratings are:
- Equalweight: future performance as expected
- Overweight: future performance better than expected

There are different kinds of ratings, and they are just opinions.

Different analysts can give different ratings for the same stock because they may have different views or data.

6 Day Course
Theme: mistakes while seeing returns

MonTueWedThuFriSun

As investors, we all like to analyse mutual funds, stocks, FDs, etc, before investing.

But investors often make mistakes when analysing investment options or when measuring returns of their own investments.

In this week’s course, we will explore some of these mistakes.

One such mistake is looking at recent returns.

Equity mutual funds and stocks do not give consistent returns. Usually, their returns are irregular.

So, if you are looking at a mutual fund's last 1 year return, there’s no guarantee that the next 1 year period will be the same.

Many investors get attracted to funds that have recently done well and start investing.

The next year, when the mutual fund does not perform similarly, they feel surprised.

Featured Question
Q. “Conventional wisdom says: Buy low P/E stocks and sell them when their P/E is high. But there are any stocks where you do the reverse. To make money in them, you must buy when their P/E is high and sell when the P/E is low. Which are these stocks?”
PE ratio tells us the ratio of a stock’s price compared to its earnings.

A high PE ratio means the stock’s price is high relative to its earnings.

Usually, such a stock is deemed overvalued.

Now, is it possible that you bought a stock with high PE ratio, and then the ratio fell but you still made money?

Yes — if the earnings rise very fast.

If the earnings rise very fast and the share price rises (but slowly), you will be making money.

In short, the company would be a high-growth company.

Usually, in the case of high-growth companies, as their earnings grow, the share price also grows.

Which means the PE ratio remains roughly the same.
