Word of the Day
Expense Ratio


It is a fee charged by fund houses to manage the mutual funds.

This charge includes fund manager fees, administrative costs, marketing expenses, etc.

It is shown as a percentage of the total assets the fund manages.

Example: if a mutual fund has an expense ratio of 1%, and you invest Rs 1 lakh, then around Rs 1,000 per year is charged for managing the funds.

This means that if your fund shows a 10% return, and the expense ratio is 1%, the returns before deducting the expense ratio is effectively 11%.

The expense ratio is deducted before the returns are shown, which means the returns you see are the returns you will actually get.

Theme: mistakes while seeing returns

MonTueWedThuFriSun

It is easy to talk about investments in terms of one starting value and one ending value.

“I invested Rs 15,000 on 1st June 2022, and withdrew on 1st June 2025 when it had grown to Rs 20,000”.

But real investments almost never look like this. We invest money every month and sometimes irregularly. And we withdraw when we need money sometimes.

This makes calculating the return of the investment very tough.

In such cases, we can use XIRR (Extended Internal Rate of Return).

XIRR accounts for all the amounts invested, all amounts withdrawn, and the duration of each investment.

This is an excellent tool to track SIP investments. It is also very useful in tracking investments in multiple mutual funds or stocks.

Many investment platforms show the XIRR returns, along with absolute returns, CAGR, etc.

Calculating XIRR on your own can be a bit challenging if you’re not used to accounting.

There are many tutorials on YouTube that show you how to do so if you are interested.

Featured Question
Q. “The cost of barrel is decreasing which should have lowered the prices of all types of products related to oil. Why is our cooking oil prices hiked?”
In practice, we do not see petrol/diesel prices change in the same manner as crude oil prices.

Many times, the barrel price of crude oil changes, but the change does not reflect in the end products.

This is because every country and state imposes taxes on crude oil products.

These taxes are tweaked and changed regularly and can directly impact the final price of that product.

Also, cooking oil is made from agricultural products. Agricultural products’ prices can change due to several reasons, including monsoons, harvest time, supply, demand for the agricultural product, etc.
