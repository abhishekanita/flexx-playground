# Skill: gold‑investing

## Title & Introduction
This skill helps Indian retail investors decide whether, how much, and in which form to own gold: Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs), Gold ETFs, Gold mutual funds / FoFs, digital/physical gold (coins, bars, jewellery), custody and taxation differences, allocation rules, and the cultural context (marriage, gifts, jewellery). It activates when a user asks about "buy gold", "SGB vs ETF", "how much gold in portfolio", "tax on gold", "physical vs paper gold", or wants help picking a product for a specific goal (marriage, emergency fund, hedge).

Actionable trigger: ask the user for (1) goal/purpose (consumption vs investment), (2) time horizon, (3) liquidity needs, and (4) current portfolio allocation — then guide product choice using the frameworks below.

## Core Rules / Golden Rules
Each rule is short, evidence‑backed and actionable. Use these as decision anchors.

1. Rule: Treat gold mainly as a hedge/diversifier — not an equities replacement.
   - Why: Community consensus: gold's role is portfolio diversification; equities still drive long‑term upside. Keep gold allocation small and explicit.
   - Evidence: "Gold's primary portfolio role in these discussions is as a hedge/portfolio diversifier (2–10%), not a long-term outperformance vehicle — equities still dominate long-term upside."
   - Hinglish quote: "Deciding on the goal is crucial, since it allows the investor to think of a proper plan."
   - Actionable takeaway: Target 2–10% of portfolio in gold depending on risk profile and goal (see Decision Frameworks).

2. Rule: For most retail investors, Gold ETFs (or FoFs) are the practical way to own gold; SGBs are for those who can accept lower liquidity and want interest.
   - Why: ETFs give market liquidity and eliminate custody hassles; SGBs pay an annual interest (~2.5%) but lock you in and have liquidity trade‑offs.
   - Evidence: "For retail investors, Gold ETFs are the practical way to own physical gold without custody hassle; SGBs are the liquidity‑tradeoff alternative that also pays ~2.5% interest."
   - Hinglish quote: "Gold ETFs are the practical way to own physical gold without custody hassle; SGBs are the liquidity‑tradeoff alternative that also pays ~2.5% interest."
   - Actionable takeaway: Prefer Gold ETFs for tradability and SIPs; choose SGBs if you want RBI-backed bonds + interest and can hold longer.

3. Rule: Choose the form of gold by primary use-case: cultural/consumption vs investment vs tax planning vs SIP convenience.
   - Why: Jewellery satisfies cultural/use needs (weddings, heirlooms) but has hidden costs; ETFs/FoFs/SGBs are investment-grade with clearer economics.
   - Evidence: Research repeatedly notes jewellery's cultural role vs investment inefficiency (making charges, lower purity, resale penalty).
   - Hinglish quote: "Now you know why people invest in real estate and gold jewelry."
   - Actionable takeaway: If you need wearable gold for ceremony/legacy, jewellery is fine — but treat it as partly consumption, not pure investment.

4. Rule: Tax & holding period differences matter — always check holding‑period‑driven LTCG treatment before you choose product.
   - Why: Holding-period rules change after 12 or 24 months and materially change effective returns on medium‑term trades.
   - Evidence: "Listed gold-backed products in India (Gold ETFs) are taxed with a 1‑year LTCG holding period, whereas many other gold forms use a 2‑year LTCG holding period — tax rules can materially change the choice between ETF and FoF."
   - Hinglish quote: "Gold ETFs in India are typically backed by standardised 1 kg bars (99.5% purity) held via a custodian->vaulting-agent chain, and AMCs perform periodic physical verification — custody risk is operational, not theoretical."
   - Actionable takeaway: If you plan to hold 12–24 months, prefer ETFs (1‑year LTCG). For >2 years, SGBs or ETFs both make tax sense depending on other factors.

5. Rule: Fees and operational costs matter for gold products — check TER, creation/redemption mechanics and liquidity before buying.
   - Why: Gold ETFs have higher TERs than equity index ETFs because of vaulting/insurance/transport. FoFs layer fees. Traded price can deviate from iNAV when liquidity is thin.
   - Evidence: "Gold ETFs carry higher expense ratios than passive equity ETFs because vaulting, insurance and transport costs are real and visible in the TER (typically ~0.30–0.80%)."
   - Hinglish quote: "The custody and logistics (vault storage, transport, insurance) create ongoing costs that appear in the fund's Total Expense Ratio (TER)."
   - Actionable takeaway: Compare TER (0.30–0.80% typical), AUM, daily traded volume and iNAV divergence before picking an ETF.

6. Rule: If you worry about counterparty/custody risk, verify the physical backing and audit process — but recognise custody risk is operational, not theoretical.
   - Why: Gold ETFs are physically backed by LBMA-style 1 kg bars and are subject to custodians and vaulting agents; AMCs perform periodic verification.
   - Evidence: "Gold ETFs in India are backed by physical 1 kg bars of 99.5% purity... stored in insured vaults" and "AMCs perform periodic physical verification."
   - Hinglish quote: "Yes. According to the AMC representative, Gold ETFs in India are backed by actual physical gold — standardised 1 kg bars of 99.5% purity stored in secure vaults with insurance."
   - Actionable takeaway: For comfort, check AMC disclosures: custodian name, vaulting agent, audit cadence, insurance cover.

## Key Concepts Explained

1. Forms of gold — quick glossary
   - Physical jewellery (22‑carat ≈ .916), coins, bars (.995/.999)
   - Gold ETFs (exchange‑listed, physically backed by 1 kg bars, trade in Demat)
   - Gold mutual funds / Fund of Funds (FoFs) — invest in Gold ETFs or gold miners
   - Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) — RBI issued, interest ≈ 2.5% p.a., issued in tranches; secondary market tradable but less liquid
   - Digital Gold / e‑gold — platform-specific (not always equivalent to ETF/SGB backing) — treat case-by-case

2. Comparison table (practical features)

| Feature / Product | Liquidity (ease to sell) | Custody | Tax holding period (LTCG) | Interest | Costs / Notes |
|---|---:|---|---:|---:|---|
| Gold ETF | High (exchange) | Custodian & vault | 1 year for LTCG | No | TER ~0.30–0.80% (vault/insurance/transport); check iNAV & daily volume |
| Gold FoF (mutual fund) | Medium (AMC redemptions) | Underlying ETFs or physical | 2 years typical | No | Two layers of cost possible; 2‑yr LTCG often applies |
| SGB (RBI) | Low–Medium (secondary market) | Govt‑backed certificate | Treated favourably on maturity (tax‑free CG on maturity per past practice; community notes tax changes have occurred — confirm current rule) | ~2.5% p.a. | Issued in tranches; early redemption rules limited; secondary market pricing can vary |
| Physical coins/bars | Low (depends buyer) | You hold | Sale treated per typical capital gains (often 2‑yr for jewellery?) | No | Making charges, GST, purity issues; resale penalty ~20% quoted |
| Jewellery | Low for pure investment | You hold/wear | Usually treated as physical; resale price lower | No | Cultural value; making charges + lower purity; "expect 20% of its value to disappear." |
| Digital gold (platform) | Variable | Depends on provider | Varies | No | Check backing, convertibility, custody, charges |

Actionable takeaways:
- Use ETFs when you want tradability + easy SIPs.
- Use SGBs if you want RBI-backed exposure + interest and can tolerate less intraday liquidity.
- Treat jewellery as cultural/consumption; do not expect investment‑grade returns.

3. How Gold ETFs are backed — operational chain
   - AMC buys physical gold (OTC) -> custodian holds it -> vaulting agent stores 1 kg LBMA‑grade bars (99.5% purity) -> insurance & periodic physical verification.
   - Costs of vaulting, insurance and transport flow into TER.
   - ETFs publish iNAV; market‑makers provide intraday liquidity.

Actionable takeaways:
- Check AMC factsheet for custodian>vault details and audit cadence.
- Compare iNAV vs traded price before placing a big order; prefer counters with healthy daily volume.

4. Tax basics (India‑specific)
   - Holding period difference: Gold ETFs (listed) typically treated with a 1‑year LTCG holding period; many other gold forms (FoF, physical?) often treated with 2‑year LTCG. (Tax rules can change; confirm before large moves.)
   - SGB interest: ~2.5% p.a. (community data). Tax treatment on maturity has been subject to concern in the community — verify latest government position for large decisions.

Actionable takeaways:
- If you expect to sell within 12–24 months, check product‑specific holding‑periods: ETFs often better for 12–24 months.
- Always verify the current tax table before making a large trade.

5. Costs that reduce returns
   - TER: Gold ETFs ~0.30–0.80% (higher than equity ETFs).
   - FoF: may pay both underlying fund TER + fund's own TER.
   - Physical jewellery: making charges, GST, dealer margins — resale value often much lower than purchase price.

Actionable takeaway:
- For investment exposure, calculate net expected return = gold price change − TER − taxes − expected bid/ask slippage.

## Common Mistakes (real examples from the research)
1. Buying jewellery expecting it to be a pure investment — ignores making charges and purity; resale hit quoted as "expect 20% of its value to disappear."
2. Choosing gold product only on headline convenience (e.g., "digital gold") without verifying backing and conversion rules.
3. Ignoring tax holding periods — buying a FoF then selling before 2 years and paying higher tax.
4. Not checking ETF liquidity & iNAV — buying at wide premium/discount or trading thinly‑traded counters.
5. Treating SGBs as instantly liquid — failing to account for secondary market illiquidity and price swings.
6. Overweighting gold after short-term outperformance (7–8 year point‑to‑point spikes) — mistaking cyclical performance for structural outperformance.
7. Using borrowed money or loans to buy gold (or any asset) — leverage is risky.
8. Failing to verify physical backing / AMC custodian details when buyer is worried about custody risk.
9. Paying unnecessary fees by using regular MF plans / FoFs instead of cheaper ETFs/direct plans.
10. Holding jewellery as emergency liquidity — hard to monetize quickly at close to metal value.

Actionable takeaways:
- Before buying: check backing, TER, tax holding period, liquidity metrics and whether purchase is for consumption vs investment.

## Decision Frameworks

1. Which gold product should I buy? (Decision tree)
   - Step 1: Define primary purpose
     - Consumption/wear (wedding/gift) → jewellery (buy from trusted jeweller; negotiate making charges; get hallmark).
     - Pure investment / hedge → go to Step 2.
   - Step 2: Time horizon & liquidity
     - <12 months, need liquidity → Gold ETF.
     - 12–24 months, tax-sensitive → Gold ETF (1‑yr LTCG) likely better than FoF.
     - >24 months, want interest & govt backing → SGB if you accept lower short‑term liquidity.
   - Step 3: SIP / recurring purchase
     - Want SIP style accumulation → Gold FoF or ETF via systematic purchase (some brokers allow periodic ETF purchases).
   - Step 4: Counterparty/custody concern
     - Concerned about custody transparency → prefer ETF (transparent AMC disclosures) or SGB (RBI‑issued).
   - Final: Always check TER, liquidity (daily volume), iNAV vs traded price, and tax holding period.

2. How much gold to allocate? (Simple rule)
   - Step 1: Start with emergency & debts done (6–12 months emergency fund).
   - Step 2: If high risk tolerance & long horizon: allocate 2–10% to gold.
   - Step 3: If goal = inflation hedge or cultural preservation (e.g., marriage), tilt allocation to 5–10% and blend jewellery + financial gold.
   - Step 4: Rebalance yearly — if gold deviates >2–3% vs target, rebalance back.

3. Sell or hold SGB/ETF after policy headlines
   - Step 1: Don’t panic sell. Check official notification and AMC communications.
   - Step 2: If liquidity needed short-term, consider selling ETF slice for quick access (ETFs trade intraday).
   - Step 3: If tax on maturity changed and you hold SGBs, compute the after-tax yield vs alternative (ETF + tax) and decide.
   - Step 4: If exposure >target, rebalance to target percentage.

Actionable takeaways:
- Use the decision tree as checklist before any trade.
- Document your goal & horizon; that alone often resolves the best product choice.

## Numbers & Benchmarks (quick reference)
- Recommended allocation range: 2–10% of portfolio (World Gold Council / community).
- Gold ETF TER: typically ~0.30%–0.80% (higher than many equity index ETFs due to vaulting/insurance/transport costs).
- Gold ETF backing: 1 kg LBMA‑standard bars, 99.5% purity (physical backing via custodian and vaulting agent).
- SGB interest: ~2.5% p.a. (community figure).
- Tax holding periods (community data):
  - Listed Gold ETFs: 1‑year LTCG holding period.
  - Many other gold forms (Gold FoF, some mutual funds, physical?): 2‑year LTCG holding period.
- ETF direct redemption safety valve (India‑specific rules):
  - Traded price discounted >1% to day‑end NAV for 7 consecutive trading days; or
  - No quotes on exchange for 3 consecutive trading days; or
  - Average total bid size < half creation unit size over 7 days → investor can request direct redemption with AMC.
- Jewellery purity & resale:
  - Typical jewellery purity ≈ .916 (22 carat). Community: "expect 20% of its value to disappear." (resale penalty due to making charges/GST/low purity).
- Emergency fund benchmark: 6–12 months of expenses (community consensus).
- 80C tax deduction limit: ₹1.5L (relevant because some investors use ELSS/equity options for tax saving in broader asset allocation).

Actionable takeaways:
- Use TER and liquidity as primary filters when selecting a Gold ETF.
- For tax-sensitive horizons between 12–24 months, prefer ETFs (1‑yr LTCG) where applicable.

## Practical Checklists & Scripts (Actionable)

1. Before buying any gold product — checklist
   - Define purpose: consumption vs investment.
   - Time horizon (months/years).
   - Check product holding‑period tax rule (ETF 1‑yr vs FoF 2‑yr per community).
   - For ETFs: check TER, daily traded volume, iNAV spread, custodian name, AUM.
   - For SGB: check tranche specifics, issue price, interest rate, secondary market liquidity.
   - For physical: check purity (.995 bars vs .916 jewellery), invoice (making charges + GST), hallmarking.
   - Action: If unsure, buy small test amount or use ETFs.

2. If you’re worried about ETF liquidity before selling
   - Check last 7 days’ traded volume and average bid size.
   - If no active market and price is >1% below NAV for 7 days, contact AMC for direct redemption.
   - Save AMC contact and factsheet; note the “direct redemption safety valve” conditions.

3. Script when verifying a seller/broker/AMC
   - "Please share the AMC factsheet link, custodian name, latest physical audit report and TER. I will confirm before I place my order."
   - For jewellery: "Please give GST invoice, carat/purity certification, and buyback policy in writing."
