## Title & Introduction
This skill covers everything an Indian retail investor needs when asking about gold: Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs), Gold ETFs & FoFs, physical gold (jewellery, coins, bars), Digital Gold, Electronic Gold Receipts (EGRs), Gold Monetisation Scheme (GMS), hallmarking/purity, gold loans, taxation, costs, recent regulatory changes (Budget 2026, SEBI & RBI circulars), and common user mistakes. It activates on queries like “Should I buy SGB or Gold ETF?”, “How is digital gold taxed?”, “Is jewellery a good investment for a wedding?”, “How to verify hallmark?”, “SGB taxation after Budget 2026?”, and practical how‑to questions (storage, selling, gold loan, converting ETF to jewellery).

Use this skill to give goal‑based, India‑specific, regulation‑aware advice with clear next steps, checks, and plain‑Hindi (Hinglish) examples.

## Core Rules / Golden Rules
Each rule is short, actionable, and backed by research.

1. Prioritise regulated products for investments
   - Actionable: Prefer SGBs, Gold ETFs, EGRs and FoFs for investment exposure; avoid unregulated digital gold for primary investment.
   - Why: SEBI issued a caution (PR No. 70/2025) about unregulated digital gold — no SEBI/RBI oversight, no formal grievance redressal, high counterparty risk and large hidden buy-sell spreads (~6%).
   - Quote: "SEBI issued a strong caution (PR No. 70/2025) against unregulated 'Digital Gold' platforms, highlighting their risks."

2. Know the new SGB tax rule — tax exemption is now narrow
   - Actionable: If you want tax-free capital gain on SGBs, buy in the primary issue and hold to full 8‑year maturity. Assume secondary-market buys or premature redemptions will be taxable.
   - Why: Budget 2026 (effective April 1, 2026) restricts SGB capital‑gains exemption to original primary subscribers who hold to maturity. Secondary purchases or premature exits are taxed (LTCG 12.5% if >12 months; STCG at slab rate if ≤12 months).
   - Quote: "A significant change from Budget 2026, effective April 1, 2026, restricts the capital gains tax exemption. The exemption now applies exclusively to individuals who purchased the SGBs during the primary issuance and hold them until the full 8-year maturity."

3. Always calculate full cost stack — taxes, spreads, GST, TER, making charges
   - Actionable: Before buying, compute: buy/sell spread, GST (3%), making charges + 5% GST on jewellery making, ETF TER (0.3%–1.0% pa), demat/DP fees, and digital-gold spreads (~6%).
   - Why: Costs materially reduce returns; physical jewellery resale can erode 15–20% (making + GST + jeweller buyback deductions). Digital gold often shows high spreads and unnecessary friction.
   - Quote: "Account for All Costs: Factor in the complete cost stack for each product."

4. Insist on hallmark & verify purity for physical gold
   - Actionable: Buy only hallmarked jewellery/coins — check the 6‑digit alphanumeric HUID on the BIS CARE app before purchase and demand certificate.
   - Why: Since March 31, 2023, sale of jewellery without HUID is prohibited in covered districts; BIS hallmark protects purity and gives recourse (compensation if shortfall).
   - Quote: "Use the 'Verify HUID' feature on the BIS CARE mobile app to confirm the item's authenticity, purity, and the jeweller's details before purchasing. Do not buy non-hallmarked gold."

5. Match product to goal and horizon
   - Actionable: Use SGBs for long-term (8+ years) TRULY locked, tax-considered holding; use Gold ETFs/Gold FoFs for SIPs, liquidity and frequent access; use physical gold only for cultural/ritual or immediate jewellery needs; digital gold only for micro-savings/gifting with awareness of risks.
   - Why: Each product has distinct liquidity, tax and cost profiles — choose by purpose, not brandability.
   - Quote: "Match the Product to Your Goal: For long-term wealth creation (8+ years) with maximum tax efficiency, SGBs purchased in the primary issue are ideal. For liquidity, transparency, and systematic investing (SIPs), Gold ETFs are the superior choice."

6. Build a liquid emergency fund before locking wealth in illiquid gold
   - Actionable: Keep 3–12 months expenses in liquid cash/liquid funds before allocating 10–15% to gold; do not rely on physical/SGBs as emergency cash.
   - Why: Physical gold and SGBs may be illiquid or taxed on exit; community cautions repeated that “physical gold and SGBs are poor substitutes for a liquid emergency fund.”
   - Quote: "Physical gold and SGBs behave as a safety/heritage asset but are poor substitutes for a liquid emergency fund."

7. When taking gold loans, check LTV, valuation method and auction rules
   - Actionable: If borrowing against gold, confirm lender’s LTV, which per RBI is tiered: up to ₹2.5L → 85% LTV; ₹2.5–5L → 80%; above ₹5L → 75%. Verify valuation source (IBJA or exchange), auction rules and borrower rights.
   - Why: RBI mandates valuation rules and auction transparency; know your rights (surplus refund within 7 working days; compensation for delayed release).
   - Quote: "If taking a loan against gold, be aware of the RBI's tiered Loan-to-Value (LTV) framework (up to 85% for loans under ₹2.5 lakh)."

8. Record everything and consult a tax advisor for large decisions
   - Actionable: Keep invoices, HUIDs, demat statements, SGB tranche details, and purchase dates; consult a CA before major moves (selling SGBs after tax-rule change, converting digital gold to physical, inheritance issues).
   - Why: Post‑Budget 2026 tax & indexation removal make capital gains calculations and confirmation of entitlement complex; documentation is essential.

## Key Concepts Explained

- Quick glossary
  - SGB — Sovereign Gold Bonds (issued by RBI on behalf of Govt. of India): 8‑year tenor, 2.5% pa interest, premature redemption after 5 years (subject to calendar), tax-exemption on capital gain only for original primary subscribers holding to maturity (Budget 2026).
  - Gold ETF — Exchange-traded fund that holds physical 99.5% gold (95–100% allocation in gold per SEBI), traded on NSE/BSE; high liquidity; TER 0.3%–1.0% pa.
  - Gold FoF — Mutual fund investing in Gold ETFs (useful if no Demat), often higher holding-period tax (2 years).
  - Physical gold — Jewellery, coins, bars; hallmark mandatory (BIS, HUID), GST applies (3% on value; 5% on making charges for jewellery).
  - Digital gold — Fractional ownership offered by unregulated platforms (SafeGold, MMTC‑PAMP, Augmont, apps like PhonePe/Paytm). SEBI PR No. 70/2025 cautions about regulatory risk and high spreads (~6%).
  - EGR — Electronic Gold Receipts: SEBI‑regulated, dematerialised receipts representing physical gold in vaults; tradable on exchanges.
  - GMS (Gold Monetisation Scheme) — RBI/Govt scheme to deposit idle gold with banks, earn interest (paid in gold or rupees); direct government scheme.

- Comparison table — practical parameters
  | Parameter | SGB (primary) | Gold ETF | Physical (jewellery/coins) | Digital Gold | EGR / GMS |
  |---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
  | Regulator | RBI (issue) / RBI rules | SEBI / AMCs | BIS (purity/hallmark) + consumer laws | Unregulated (SEBI warning PR No.70/2025) | SEBI (EGR) / RBI (GMS) |
  | Backing | Govt promise + gold-linked | 95–100% physical gold (99.5%) | Actual metal | Claimed physical backing (third-party vaults) | Physical gold held in vaults |
  | Interest | 2.5% p.a. (taxable as income) | None | None | None | GMS pays interest (varies) |
  | Liquidity | Low; tradable on secondary market | High (exchange) | Low; resale deductions | Medium (depends on platform buy/sell) | EGR: exchange liquidity; GMS: locked till tenure |
  | Holding min | Primary tranche sizes (issue) | Fractional units | Any coin weight / jewellery | ₹1 micro | EGR demat unit sizes |
  | Tax on LTCG (post-Jul 23, 2024 & rules) | Exempt only if primary subscriber & held to 8-year maturity (Budget 2026); secondary/premature taxed LTCG 12.5% (>12 months) | LTCG 12.5% (>12 months) without indexation | LTCG 12.5% (>24 months) without indexation | LTCG 12.5% (>24 months) without indexation | EGR taxed similar to ETFs/EGR rules; GMS interest/cap gains may be exempt per scheme |
  | GST | Exempt | No GST on exchange units | 3% on value + 5% on making charges | 3% on purchase | EGR: no GST if traded on exchange |
  | Costs / Friction | No TER; bid/offer premiums on secondary | TER 0.3%–1% + DP/brokerage | Making charges (10–25%) + GST; storage/insurance | Buy-sell spread ~6% + GST 3% | Custody/transaction costs |

- Holding period & taxation (concise)
  - Gold ETFs: LTCG if held >12 months → 12.5% (no indexation) for assets transferred on/after July 23, 2024.
  - Physical Gold / Digital Gold / Gold Mutual Funds (FoFs): LTCG if >24 months → 12.5% (no indexation).
  - SGBs: interest 2.5% tax as income (slab); capital gains on redemption tax-exempt ONLY for primary subscribers who hold full 8 years (Budget 2026); otherwise LTCG 12.5% if >12 months (secondary market or premature).
  - GST: 3% on physical & digital gold purchases; 5% GST on making charges for jewellery. SGB & Gold ETF units bought on exchange are GST-exempt.

- Hallmarking & purity
  - Mandatory hallmark components (BIS): BIS logo, purity/fineness grade (14K, 18K, 20K, 22K916, 23K, 24K), 6‑digit alphanumeric HUID.
  - Verify HUID: BIS CARE mobile app → Verify HUID feature; consumer entitlements include compensation equal to two times the value of calculated shortage if purity test fails.
  - Practical purity tip: For investment coins/bars prefer .995/.999 purity and buy from primary dealers or government outlets to reduce premium — "Buy only .995 purity coins of the highest weight you can afford. That too from a primary dealer." (verbatim Reddit quote)

- SEBI & valuation change (Apr 1, 2026)
  - From April 1, 2026 SEBI circular HO/(68)2026-IMD-POD-2/I/5780/2026 mandates mutual funds must value physical gold/silver using Indian exchange 'polled spot prices' — improves domestic price transparency and NAV accuracy.

- Gold loans (RBI rules & borrower rights)
  - LTV tiers (effective Apr 1, 2026): up to ₹2.5L → 85%; ₹2.5L–₹5L → 80%; above ₹5L → 75%.
  - Valuation: lower of 30‑day average 22‑carat price or previous day closing price from IBJA or SEBI-recognized exchange; only gold weight counts (exclude stones/making).
  - Auction rules: public notice in 2 newspapers (one vernacular), reserve price min 90% market value initially; surplus proceeds to borrower within 7 working days; compensation ₹5,000/day for delayed collateral release beyond 7 days.
  - Actionable: if taking a gold loan, ask lender to show LTV, valuation source, auction terms in writing.

- Electronic Gold Receipts (EGR)
  - SEBI-regulated, demat form, convertible to physical gold on request. Useful for regulated, transparent, demat-holding investors.

## Common Mistakes (real, drawn from research)
1. Treating jewellery as an investment rather than cultural/consumption item
   - "Avoid jewellery at all cost , when you go to sell expect 20 percent of its value to disappear" (verbatim).
   - Result: big implicit loss when converting to cash.

2. Using digital gold as a primary investment without understanding counterparty risk and 6% spreads
   - Consequence: high friction and invisible cost erosion.

3. Misreading SGB tax rules and buying on secondary market expecting maturity exemption
   - Many were surprised by Budget 2026; buyers assumed tax-free maturity even for secondary purchases.

4. Underestimating total cost for physical gold (making charges + 5% GST on making)
   - Example: making charges difference materially changes final cost for jewellery conversion.

5. Not verifying hallmark/HUID before purchase
   - Leads to purity disputes and difficulty in recourse.

6. No emergency fund while holding illiquid gold/SGB-heavy portfolio
   - Illiquidity forces distress sales or high-cost loans.

7. Buying SGBs or bonds with no plan for interim liquidity needs
   - Many SGBs are illiquid and have issue/maturity constraints.

8. Ignoring expense ratios and tracking error differences between Gold ETFs
   - Two ETFs tracking gold can diverge materially due to TER & liquidity.

9. Borrowing to invest (using loans or margin to buy gold or stocks)
   - Community caution: “Never borrow to invest” — margin amplifies losses.

10. Panic-selling after a regulatory/tax change without verifying facts
    - Reactionary selling often crystallises tax liabilities and losses.

## Decision Frameworks

1. Which gold product should I buy? (Goal-based decision tree)
   - Step 1: Why do you want gold?
     - A) Investment / portfolio diversification (hedge) → go to Step 2
     - B) Jewellery for wedding/rituals → go to Step 3
     - C) Small gifting / micro-saving → go to Step 4
     - D) Short-term liquidity / collateral → go to Step 5
   - Step 2 (Investment): Horizon & tax
     - Horizon ≥ 8 years and you can buy primary SGB → consider SGB primary (note: tax exemption only if held to maturity). If you need SIPs/liquidity → Gold ETF (SIP/ETF). If you want demat-free route → Gold FoF.
   - Step 3 (Jewellery): Cultural value
     - Need jewellery now for rituals → buy hallmarked jewellery (minimum, lightweight design to reduce making charges); consider borrowing relatives for large pieces. If you can delay, accumulate ETFs/coins and convert 2–3 months before event.
   - Step 4 (Gifting/Micro-saves):
     - Small amounts (₹1–₹1,000/month) → Digital Gold acceptable for gifting but be aware of platform risk and spreads; better regulated choice: Gold ETF SIP in small amounts (needs Demat/trading account) or 1g coins at intervals.
   - Step 5 (Collateral/loans):
     - Need against a loan → physical hallmarked gold acceptable; verify lender LTV and auction terms as per RBI. Avoid pledging jewellery with stones (only gold weight counts).
   - Final check: Compute all costs (GST, making, TER, spread), verify regulatory status (SGB primary? Gold ETF custodian? Digital platform regulated?), and document purchase proofs.

2. SGB: Sell, Hold or Panic? (after Budget 2026)
   - Step 1: Are you an original primary subscriber who will hold to full 8-year maturity?
     - Yes → expected exemption on maturity (per current Budget 2026 rule) — hold unless you need liquidity.
     - No (secondary purchase / will redeem early) → treat as taxable (LTCG 12.5% if >12 months): calculate after-tax proceeds vs alternative. Consider:
       - If shortage of cash → sell only if after-tax proceeds meet need.
       - If market price high and tax hit large → consider hold or staggered sell to manage LTCG bite.
   - Step 2: Consult broker/AMC/RBI clarifications and CA before decisive action.

3. Wedding gold purchase plan (timeline decision tree)
   - T‑12+ months: Start ETF/coin SIP for accumulation
   - T‑6 months: Decide which ritual pieces must be physical; plan converting some ETF/coins to coins/bars
   - T‑2 months: Buy physical coins from primary dealer if converting; avoid last‑minute heavy-making jewellery
   - T‑0 (event): Buy minimal pieces and/or borrow family jewellery

4. Gold loan decision tree (should you take one?)
   - Need for short-term liquidity?
     - Yes → prefer gold loan if interest < alternative (personal loan) and LTV acceptable. Check RBI LTV tiers; confirm valuation methodology and auction rules.
     - No → avoid pledging long-term sentimental jewellery; consider other options (balance transfer, sell liquid ETFs).
   - Always: Get loan terms in writing, verify surplus refund/auction rules.

## Numbers & Benchmarks (quick reference)

- SGB:
  - Interest: 2.5% p.a. (taxable as Income from Other Sources)
  - Tenor: 8 years (premature redemption after 5 years subject to RBI calendar)
  - Tax: Capital gains tax exemption at maturity ONLY for original primary subscribers who hold to 8 years (Budget 2026, effective Apr 1, 2026). Secondary market or premature redemptions → taxed (LTCG 12.5% >12 months).
- Gold ETFs:
  - Typical TER: 0.3%–1.0% p.a.
  - Backing: 99.5% purity, 1kg standardized bars
  - LTCG holding period: >12 months → 12.5% (no indexation) for assets transferred on/after Jul 23, 2024

  - GST on purchase: 3% of value
  - Additional GST on making charges (jewellery): 5%

- Hallmark:
  - Mandatory 6‑digit alphanumeric HUID for jewellery (since Mar 31, 2023 in covered districts)
  - Verify via BIS CARE app
- Taxation – summary:
  - LTCG on gold assets transferred on/after Jul 23, 2024 → flat 12.5% without indexation (holding periods vary by product as above)
  - STCG → taxed at individual slab rates
  - GST: 3% on physical/digital gold; 5% on making charges for jewellery
- Costs to factor:
  - Physical jewellery: making charges 10–25% typical (varies); resale deductions 10–20% potential; storage/insurance/locker fees
  - Digital gold: buy‑sell spread ~6% + 3% GST (on purchase)

## Actionable Checklists & Templates

1. Buying physical gold (quick checklist)
   - Verify hallmark HUID (6-digit) and check BIS CARE app.
   - Ask for purity (karat/fineness) and invoice with HUID printed.
   - Compute total cost = metal value + making charges + 5% GST (on making) + 3% GST (on metal if applicable).
   - For coins/bars: prefer .995/.999 purity and primary dealers or govt outlets (MMTC‑PAMP).
   - For jewellery: prefer lightweight designs and compare local jewellers vs branded.
   - Insure and store properly (bank locker or insured safe).

2. Buying Gold ETF (quick checklist)
   - Open Demat & trading account (Zerodha/Groww/etc).
   - Check ETF TER, AUM and average daily volume (liquidity).
   - Place order at market/limit; monitor iNAV for fair pricing.
   - Consider SIP for rupee-cost averaging.
   - Track DP charges and brokerage on sale.

3. Checking Digital Gold platform
   - Confirm regulatory status (SEBI/RBI registered? — most are NOT).
   - Ask about physical vault custodian, insurance, audit frequency, buy-sell spread.
   - Calculate effective cost (spread + 3% GST).
   - Use only for micro-saves/gifting; avoid for long-term core exposure.

4. If you have SGBs and worry about Budget 2026 change
   - Verify: Did you buy in primary issue? When is maturity? Do you intend to hold to 8 years?
   - Get tranche details from broker/NSDL/AMC; ask RBI/AMC FAQ for clarification.
   - Consult CA for tax implications of sale vs hold (after-tax proceeds).
   - Avoid immediate panic-sell; model after-tax outcomes first.

5. If you want to take a gold loan
   - Ask lender for LTV percentage, valuation basis (IBJA/exchange), interest rate, penalties, and auction process.
   - Get terms in writing.
   - Calculate net amount received vs amount repayable and compare with alternative borrowing costs.
